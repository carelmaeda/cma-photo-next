I don't shoot RAW. Haven't for a long time.

Every photo on this site is a JPG, finished inside the camera the moment I pressed the shutter. People assume that's a handicap. For me it was the unlock.

## The backlog was the problem

RAW promises you can decide later.

So I decided later. Hundreds of files at a time, hunched over Lightroom, re-making the same choices about warmth and contrast — slightly differently every session. My photos from the same trip didn't even match each other.

The work I was proudest of wasn't the work I edited hardest. It was the frames where I'd gotten the light right in the first place.

## Deciding before, not after

Shooting JPG moves every decision to before the shutter.

The look lives in the camera as a [recipe](/downloads) — mine renders warm shadows and soft highlights, so I find light that suits it. If the highlights are going to clip, I see it on the back of the camera *while I can still do something about it*: move, wait, recompose.

That feedback loop is the real difference. RAW lets you fix mistakes. JPG teaches you to stop making them.

## What I give up

Honesty matters here: JPG is less forgiving.

Blow the exposure by two stops and there's no slider that brings it back. Mixed light that fights the recipe is just hard. Some frames die that RAW would have saved.

I found that an acceptable price. Limitation makes me more creative — the same reason I shoot [one prime lens](/blog/one-camera-one-lens) and let film simulate nothing on the Leica.

## If you want to try it

Don't start cold. Set your camera to RAW+JPG, load a recipe, and shoot a week while only looking at the JPGs.

When you notice you haven't opened a RAW file in a month, you'll know.

The [London album](/gallery/london) is what this practice looks like over a trip — every frame straight out of camera, on [gear that fits in one small bag](/about).
