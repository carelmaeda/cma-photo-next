Vanilla Summer is the recipe I kept coming back to until it stopped being an experiment and became the way my cameras see.

Warm shadows. Soft highlights. That feeling of 4pm light, even at noon.

The [full settings come on a recipe card](/downloads) — this piece is about *why* they're set the way they are, because a recipe you understand is one you can adapt.

## The goal

I wanted summer afternoon light to be the default, not the exception.

Not orange. Not a filter. Just the gentle warmth that makes you remember a place better than it probably was.

That meant three decisions, and every setting serves one of them.

## Decision one: the shadows go warm

Most digital cameras render shadows slightly cool — technically accurate, emotionally flat.

The white balance shift pulls the shadow end toward warmth. It's a small move. You shouldn't notice it directly; you should just feel that the frame is friendly.

This is the heart of the recipe. If you take one idea from it, take this: WB shift is where the mood lives.

## Decision two: the highlights stay soft

Hard, clipped highlights are the fastest way to make a photo look digital.

So the highlight tone rolls off early and gently — closer to how negative film gives up its brights. Windows glow instead of burning. Skies keep their last bit of color.

The trade: you give up some punch. I found that I never missed it.

## Decision three: nothing shouts

Color sits restrained. Grain is there, but you have to look for it. Sharpness stays human.

Every setting that could call attention to itself is turned down one notch from where you'd expect. The subject of the photo should be the place, not the look.

## Loading it

The recipe targets X-Trans IV and V bodies. The [full settings come on a recipe card](/downloads), with a loading note — set it to a custom slot, then forget the menus.
