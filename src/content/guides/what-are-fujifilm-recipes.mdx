A Fujifilm recipe is a saved combination of in-camera settings that gives every JPG a consistent look — before the file ever leaves the camera.

No Lightroom. No presets. No editing afterwards.

The photo you see on the back of the camera is the photo.

## The ingredients

Every recipe is built from the same handful of menus:

- **Film Simulation** — Fujifilm's starting points, modeled on their film stocks. This sets the base character.
- **White balance and WB shift** — the biggest lever most people never touch. A small shift changes the entire temperature of a look.
- **Highlight and shadow tone** — how hard the bright and dark ends roll off. Soft highlights read like film. Crushed shadows read like drama.
- **Color** — saturation, but gentler than the slider you're imagining.
- **Grain, Color Chrome, clarity** — the finishing touches.

Change a few of these together, save them to a custom slot, and you have a recipe. One dial click and the camera shoots that look all day.

## Why bother?

I found that the editing step was where my photos went to die.

Hundreds of RAW files, a backlog of guilt, and a look that drifted every time I opened the laptop. Recipes removed the entire step. Now the decisions happen once — in the menus, slowly, deliberately — and then I just shoot.

There's a second effect I didn't expect: committing to a look changes how you see. When you know your shadows fall warm, you start hunting for the light that flatters them. The recipe stops being a filter and becomes a way of looking.

## Where to start

If you shoot any X-series or GFX body, you already have everything you need.

Start with one film simulation you like. Shoot it for a week without touching anything else. Then move one setting at a time — WB shift first, it teaches you the most.

Or start from a recipe someone else refined and adjust from there. Mine is called [Vanilla Summer](/downloads) — warm shadows, soft highlights, that 4pm-light feeling. It's free, and it ships in both X-mount and GFX versions.
