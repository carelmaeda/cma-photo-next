Most days I leave the house with one camera and no bag.

The X100V has a fixed 23mm lens — you couldn't change it if you wanted to. On the X-Pro3, the XF 35mm f/1.4 has been glued on for years. On a trip, the GFX100RF comes along, also one lens.

No zoom. No "what should I bring" hour the night before. One camera, one field of view, done.

## Zooming with your feet is a cliché — and true

When the frame is too tight, I step back. Too loose, I walk in.

That sounds like a downgrade from twisting a zoom ring. It isn't, because moving changes the *perspective*, not just the crop. Three steps forward makes the foreground swell and the background drop away. A zoom only makes things bigger.

After a few months at one focal length, you stop thinking about it entirely. You see the frame before the camera reaches your eye, because you already know what the lens will see.

## The decisions disappear

Every option you carry is a decision you'll have to make at the worst possible moment — when the light is doing something and you have eight seconds.

I've made my decisions in advance:

- One focal length, so framing is instinct.
- One [recipe](/downloads), so the look is settled.
- JPG only, so there's [no editing waiting at home](/blog/why-i-shoot-jpeg-only).

What's left is the only decision that matters: where to stand, and when to press.

## The honest downsides

Some photos don't happen. The bird stays a speck. The compressed mountain-behind-the-village shot belongs to someone with a 200mm.

I let those photos go. They were never mine.

## Try it cheaply

You don't need to buy anything. Pick one prime you own — or tape your zoom at one length — and commit for thirty days. Not "mostly." Every frame.
